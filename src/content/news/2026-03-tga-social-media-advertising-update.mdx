In November 2025, the Therapeutic Goods Administration updated its guidance on advertising therapeutic goods via social media platforms, clarifying the obligations that apply to businesses, health practitioners, and social media influencers promoting therapeutic goods online.

The update is significant for Australian medical practitioners. It makes clear that the reach of TGA advertising law extends well beyond a caption on an Instagram post — and that the way content is distributed, shared, or amplified on social platforms can itself constitute advertising subject to full regulatory obligations.

SafePost has updated its compliance rules database to reflect this guidance. Here is what you need to know.

## Why the TGA acted

The TGA updated its guidance to address the increase in unlawful advertising on social media, in which businesses, health services and social media influencers are promoting prescription-only medicines such as Ozempic and cosmetic injectables including Botox. These posts often use images, nicknames, hashtags and influencer-style endorsements to promote the goods.

In the 2024–25 financial year, the TGA requested the removal of over 13,700 unlawful advertisements from digital platforms. Enforcement is active and the consequences are real — including infringement notices, civil penalties, and in serious cases, criminal proceedings.

## Six things that now have explicit regulatory coverage

### 1. Influencer and affiliate arrangements

If you pay, gift, or reward someone to promote your therapeutic goods content, their posts are your advertising. Social media posts about therapeutic goods disseminated by, or on behalf of, a person with a financial interest in the sale of the goods are more likely to be considered promotional. TGA rules apply in full.

### 2. Hashtags, reposts, stories, and reels

TGA advertising rules apply to social media posts, comments and hashtags, blogs, discussion forums and streaming services. Using platform amplification mechanics — including reels, stories, sponsored ads, and closed groups — in connection with therapeutic goods content is advertising activity subject to TGA requirements.

### 3. User-generated testimonials and comments

Third-party reviews and comments about therapeutic goods are likely to be considered testimonials and must comply with the Therapeutic Goods Advertising Code. Business owners should actively monitor their channels to remove unlawful posts. Leaving non-compliant comments visible on a page you control may be treated as publishing or endorsing that content.

### 4. Private groups and closed-channel dissemination

The TGA's position is unambiguous: the same rules apply to a business' posts on its own social media accounts, posts it pays for, and advertising by any other person on platforms it controls. Promoting therapeutic goods in a private Facebook group or closed messaging channel does not reduce your regulatory obligations.

### 5. AI-generated advertising content

The method used to create advertising content does not affect the obligations that apply to it. AI-generated or AI-assisted content promoting therapeutic goods remains fully subject to TGA advertising requirements.

### 6. Adverse event capture from social channels

Business owners are responsible for the content of any social media page they create or manage, including third-party comments. Adverse event mentions appearing in comments on therapeutic goods content you control may trigger moderation and escalation obligations under TGA pharmacovigilance requirements.

## The broader principle practitioners should internalise

Whether content constitutes advertising depends on its overall effect, not how it is labelled or the intent behind it. Content presented as education, professional discussion or personal experience may still be considered advertising if it draws attention to, or promotes, the use or supply of a therapeutic good.

This is the most important takeaway from the November 2025 update. A post framed as a personal experience, an educational explainer, or a patient success story can still be advertising. The question is not what you called it — it is what it does.

## What you should do now

1. Review your existing social media content — including historical posts — for compliance with the updated guidance. The TGA has explicitly encouraged all advertisers to do this.
2. Audit any influencer or ambassador arrangements involving therapeutic goods and ensure all content meets TGA advertising requirements.
3. Establish a comment moderation process for any social media accounts where therapeutic goods are referenced.
4. Run your upcoming content through SafePost before publishing — the updated rules are live now.
