Social media has become an essential part of modern medical practice. Whether you're sharing health tips on Instagram, engaging with patients on Facebook, or building your professional profile on LinkedIn, your online presence matters — both for your practice and your reputation.

But for Australian medical practitioners, social media comes with a layer of regulatory risk that many doctors don't fully appreciate. AHPRA and the TGA have clear rules about what you can and can't say online, and the consequences of getting it wrong can be serious.

Here's what you need to know.

## What is AHPRA's advertising guidance?

AHPRA regulates advertising of regulated health services under the Health Practitioner Regulation National Law Act 2009 (the National Law). Section 133 of the National Law sets out strict requirements for how health practitioners can advertise their services.

Under these rules, advertising must not:

- Be false, misleading or deceptive
- Offer a gift, discount or other inducement to attract patients without clearly stating the terms and conditions
- Use testimonials or endorsements from patients
- Create unreasonable expectations of beneficial treatment
- Directly or indirectly encourage the unnecessary use of health services

These rules apply to every registered health practitioner in Australia — GPs, specialists, dentists, physiotherapists, chiropractors, psychologists, pharmacists, nurses and more.

## What counts as advertising under AHPRA?

This is where many practitioners are caught off guard. AHPRA's definition of "advertising" is much broader than most doctors expect.

Any communication that is designed to promote your services to the public counts as advertising — including:

- Instagram and Facebook posts
- LinkedIn articles and posts
- Your practice website
- Google Business Profile content
- YouTube videos
- Podcast appearances where you promote your services
- Email newsletters sent to patients

If your post mentions your practice, your specialty, or your services in any promotional way, AHPRA considers it advertising. That casual Instagram post about your clinic's new opening hours? Advertising. The before-and-after photo you shared on your cosmetic practice Facebook page? Advertising — and almost certainly non-compliant.

## The 5 most common AHPRA social media violations

### 1. Patient testimonials and reviews

This is the most common violation. Sharing a patient's positive review on your social media — even if the patient gave permission — breaches Section 133 of the National Law. This includes screenshot reviews, quoted testimonials, and video endorsements. Many practitioners don't realise that simply reposting a Google review to your Instagram story is a breach.

### 2. Before and after photos

Before and after images are prohibited in health service advertising unless very specific conditions are met. In cosmetic medicine this is particularly common — images showing weight loss results, skin treatment outcomes, or surgical results are almost always non-compliant.

### 3. Misleading treatment claims

Statements that create unreasonable expectations about outcomes — "guaranteed results", "100% success rate", "the best treatment for X" — breach the National Law regardless of whether you believe them to be true. Even subtle wording can be problematic.

### 4. Discounts and special offers

Offering a discount, gift or promotional incentive to attract patients is prohibited unless the full terms and conditions are clearly stated. "20% off Botox this month" without complete terms is a breach.

### 5. Misleading use of titles

Using titles or qualifications in a way that could mislead the public about your training or scope of practice is prohibited. This is particularly relevant for practitioners who have completed short courses in areas like cosmetic medicine and may inadvertently imply specialist-level qualifications.

## What about TGA rules?

The Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA) adds another layer of compliance for practitioners who advertise anything involving medicines, medical devices, or therapeutic goods.

Under the Therapeutic Goods Act 1989, prescription medicines cannot be advertised to the general public — full stop. This means any social media post that names, promotes, or implies the use of a prescription medicine to a consumer audience is potentially a serious TGA breach.

This is particularly relevant for practitioners in:

- Weight management (GLP-1 medications like semaglutide and tirzepatide)
- Cosmetic medicine (anti-wrinkle injections, dermal fillers)
- Mental health (discussing specific psychiatric medications)
- Pain management

Even educational content about prescription medicines can cross the line if it could reasonably be seen as promotional. When in doubt, don't name the medicine.

## What happens if you get it wrong?

AHPRA takes advertising compliance seriously and has significantly increased its enforcement activity in recent years. A complaint can be made by anyone — a competitor, a patient, a member of the public, or even a journalist.

If AHPRA receives a complaint about your advertising, the process typically involves:

- A formal notification that a complaint has been received
- A request for your response
- An investigation that can take months
- Outcomes ranging from no further action to conditions on your registration, a formal reprimand, or in serious cases, suspension

Beyond AHPRA, TGA breaches can result in significant financial penalties — up to hundreds of thousands of dollars for serious violations.

The reputational damage from a public AHPRA investigation can far outweigh any benefit from the non-compliant post.

## How to stay compliant on social media

Staying compliant doesn't mean abandoning social media — it means being thoughtful about what you post. Here are practical steps:

Before you post, ask yourself:

- Does this post promote my services to the public?
- Does it include any patient testimonials or outcomes?
- Does it mention a prescription medicine or restricted therapeutic good?
- Could it create unreasonable expectations about treatment results?
- Is every claim I'm making accurate and not misleading?

**Build a review process.** Before publishing any promotional content, have it reviewed — either by a colleague, your medical defence organisation, or a compliance tool.

**Keep records.** Document your compliance review process. If a complaint is ever made, demonstrating that you had a reasonable process in place matters.

**Stay updated.** AHPRA updates its advertising guidance periodically. Make sure you're reading communications from your professional association and medical defence organisation.
