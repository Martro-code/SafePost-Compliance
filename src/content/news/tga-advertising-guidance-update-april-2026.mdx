If you advertise any service that involves medicines, medical devices, or other therapeutic goods, the TGA's latest guidance update is essential reading.

On 7 April 2026, the Therapeutic Goods Administration updated its guidance on determining when content about therapeutic goods and vaping goods is regulated as advertising. The update provides a clearer framework, new practical examples, and an expanded list of factors that indicate when messaging is promotional — and therefore subject to TGA advertising requirements.

## What the update covers

The updated guidance — published at [tga.gov.au](https://www.tga.gov.au/resources/guidance/determining-if-your-content-advertising) — does not change the underlying law. What it does is explain more clearly how the TGA assesses whether particular content amounts to advertising under the *Therapeutic Goods Act 1989*.

The guidance emphasises that this assessment is made on a case-by-case basis and that no single factor is determinative. What matters is whether the content — on its face — appears designed or calculated to promote the use or supply of therapeutic goods, regardless of the publisher's actual intent.

This is a critical point for Australian health practitioners: **the TGA does not assess your subjective intention**. If a reasonable consumer would interpret your content as promoting a therapeutic good, it is likely to be treated as advertising.

## The broad definition of advertising

The TGA's definition of "advertise" is intentionally wide. It includes any statement, pictorial representation or design — across any medium — that is intended, directly or indirectly, to promote the use or supply of therapeutic goods or vaping goods.

This covers:
- Social media posts and stories
- Practice websites and landing pages
- Email newsletters and direct communications
- Blogs, articles and educational content
- Podcasts, webinars and videos
- Point-of-sale materials and signage
- Business names and trading names

The guidance explicitly includes user-generated content — meaning comments, testimonials and reviews on your platforms can also constitute advertising that you are responsible for.

## New examples from the TGA

The updated guidance includes practical examples that illustrate how the assessment works in practice. Two are particularly instructive for health practitioners:

**Example 1 — Advertising where intent is clear**

A pharmacy website lists complementary medicines for sale, including the name of each medicine and its accepted indications. Regardless of whether the site has a "buy now" function, the TGA considers this advertising that must comply with the advertising legislation.

**Example 2 — Non-promotional information**

A peer-reviewed article in a scientific journal evaluates the effectiveness of omega-3 supplementation using data from a controlled trial. It presents both positive and negative findings, acknowledges methodological limitations, and avoids persuasive or emotive language. Because it is analytical and informational in nature — not designed to promote use or supply — it is not considered advertising.

**Example 3 — How context changes the assessment**

A website that sells medical devices publishes articles written by a health professional. The articles do not mention specific products, but describe the benefits of certain devices for certain health conditions. Because the website sells those devices, the articles are likely to be considered part of the advertising for those goods. The same article published in a newsletter for health professionals, in a different context, may not be considered advertising at all.

This last example is particularly important: **context determines classification**. The same words can be advertising in one setting and non-promotional education in another.

## Factors that indicate promotional intent

The updated guidance sets out an expanded list of factors that suggest content is promotional. Health practitioners and practices should review their digital content against these indicators:

- **Calls to action** — prompting consumers to make a purchase or seek out a product
- **Favourable impressions** — referring to specific goods in a way that creates positive awareness or raises brand profile
- **Therapeutic claims** — suggesting a specific good is beneficial for a particular health condition
- **Persuasive language** — emotive adjectives ("brilliant", "life changing", "breakthrough") or superlatives ("the best", "effective", "works faster")
- **Benefit emphasis without risk balance** — highlighting positive aspects while omitting or downplaying risks or limitations
- **Purchase information** — price, availability, offers, discounts or competitions
- **Financial interest** — content about goods disseminated by someone with a financial interest in their sale

None of these factors is conclusive on its own. But the more of these elements that are present in a piece of content, the more likely it is to be assessed as advertising.

## What is not advertising

The guidance also clarifies what falls outside the definition. Non-promotional material includes:

- Disease education content that meets the TGA's specific guidance for that category
- Health service advertising that does not refer — directly or indirectly — to therapeutic goods or vaping goods
- Operational company information that does not promote specific goods
- Correspondence answering a specific, unsolicited question from a customer

The third category is particularly relevant for practice websites. A page describing your consultation process, team credentials, or clinic location — with no reference to specific therapeutic goods — is generally not advertising under the TGA framework.

## Who is responsible

The updated guidance reinforces that responsibility is broader than many practitioners assume. You are responsible for advertising if:

- You publish the content yourself
- The content is published on your behalf or at your request
- You have control over the content — including content published by staff, marketing agencies, or third parties acting on your instructions

This means outsourcing your social media or digital marketing does not outsource your compliance obligations. If your agency publishes content that promotes therapeutic goods non-compliantly, you remain responsible.

## What this means for your practice

The April 2026 update does not introduce new obligations — it clarifies existing ones. But the additional examples and expanded list of promotional indicators make it easier to self-assess content before publishing.

For any Australian health practitioner whose services involve therapeutic goods — whether that is cosmetic injectables, weight management medicines, medical devices, supplements, or any other regulated product — this guidance is now the primary reference point for understanding when your content crosses into regulated advertising territory.

The safest approach remains: if you are uncertain whether content promotes therapeutic goods, treat it as advertising and ensure it complies with the Therapeutic Goods Advertising Code before publishing.
