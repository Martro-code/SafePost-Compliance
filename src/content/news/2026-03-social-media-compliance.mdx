## Your Practice Account: Stay Visible, Stay Compliant

With 82% of Australians active on social media, your patients are already online — looking for information, reassurance, and a sense of who their doctor actually is. That's an opportunity, not a threat. You just need to show up with the right content.

**What works:**

- **Health awareness campaigns** — Seasonal reminders, national health weeks, and preventive care prompts (flu vaccines, skin checks, child immunisations) are safe territory. Keep it factual and general.
- **Everyday health tips** — Practical advice like sun safety, hydration, or the importance of regular check-ups positions your practice as helpful and approachable without crossing into personal treatment advice.
- **Behind-the-scenes content** — Team introductions, clinic milestones, and community involvement humanise your practice and build trust before a patient ever walks through the door.
- **Myth busting** — Correcting health misinformation with accurate, evidence-based content establishes you as a credible voice and genuinely helps your community.
- **Service reminders** — It's fine to let patients know what you offer. "We provide flu vaccinations — call to book" is compliant. "Best flu shot in town — guaranteed to keep you healthy all winter" is not.

**What to avoid on your clinic account:**

- Patient testimonials or success stories — prohibited under AHPRA guidelines, even with consent
- Outcome promises or exaggerated claims
- Time-limited promotional offers or tactics such as "50% off this week only"
- Anything that could be interpreted as pressuring patients into a decision
- Guaranteeing how medicines, devices, supplements, or any other products will work for patients

## Your Personal Account: The Higher-Risk Zone

Here's what surprises most practitioners: personal posts are actually responsible for more AHPRA investigations than professional ones.

"Most of the cases that have gone to tribunals about social media use have been from the practitioner's personal, not professional use," says David Gardner, a lawyer and former AHPRA investigator. "A lot of people don't appreciate that just because it's not a professional post, doesn't mean AHPRA won't care — because they very much will."

**Seven things that can get you into trouble:**

1. **Extreme views** — Threatening, racist, or sexist content has ended careers. Registration has been cancelled for online conduct that falls far outside expected professional standards — even when posted from a personal account.<sup>[1](#sources)</sup>
2. **Sweeping statements about groups** — Dozens of Australian doctors have faced AHPRA scrutiny over social media posts about the Israel-Gaza conflict.<sup>[2](#sources)</sup> Criticising a government or political group may be permitted if accurate and measured; criticising people based on race, religion, or ethnicity is not.
3. **Friending patients** — This blurs professional boundaries and can trigger a complaint.
4. **Unsubstantiated medical opinions** — There's a difference between nuanced clinical discussion and statements like "the government is out to kill you." The latter is genuinely high risk.
5. **Criticising colleagues** — One Victorian GP was deregistered partly for disparaging posts about colleagues and their workplace.<sup>[3](#sources)</sup>
6. **Fringe political content** — Publicly supporting extreme political positions puts you at greater regulatory risk, full stop.
7. **Flirting with patients** — Recent changes to health practitioner regulation law mean even a flirtatious message to a patient could now meet the threshold for sexual misconduct.<sup>[4](#sources)</sup>

## The Golden Rule

Anything you post online lasts forever. Even deleted posts can be screenshotted and surface years later — potentially in a tribunal, on a big screen, with a judge asking you to explain them.

Before you post anything — on your clinic page or your personal account — ask yourself: does this reflect the values in the [AHPRA Code of Conduct](https://www.medicalboard.gov.au/codes-guidelines-policies/code-of-conduct.aspx)?
