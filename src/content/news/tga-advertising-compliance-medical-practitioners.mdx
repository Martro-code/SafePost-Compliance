Most Australian medical practitioners know the TGA as the body that approves medicines and medical devices. Fewer realise that the TGA also governs how those products can be advertised — and that the rules apply directly to doctors, specialists, and allied health professionals who promote services involving therapeutic goods.

Getting it wrong isn't just an administrative headache. TGA advertising breaches can result in formal complaints, enforceable undertakings, and significant reputational damage. With social media making it easier than ever to reach patients directly, the compliance risks have never been higher — or less well understood.

This guide breaks down what the TGA advertising framework actually requires of medical practitioners, where practitioners most commonly trip up, and how to build compliant marketing habits into your practice.

## What is the TGA and what does it regulate?

The Therapeutic Goods Administration is Australia's regulatory authority for therapeutic goods — a category that includes prescription medicines, over-the-counter medicines, medical devices, biologicals, and complementary medicines.

The TGA sits within the Department of Health and Aged Care and operates under the *Therapeutic Goods Act 1989*. Its remit covers the approval, registration, and ongoing monitoring of therapeutic goods available in Australia — but it also has a substantial role in regulating how those goods are promoted.

For medical practitioners, this means that any communication that promotes a therapeutic good — whether on a website, in a patient brochure, on Instagram, or via email — may fall within the TGA's advertising framework, regardless of whether you manufactured or sell that product yourself.

## What is the TGA Advertising Code?

The *Therapeutic Goods Advertising Code 2021* (the Code) is the primary instrument governing the advertising of therapeutic goods in Australia. It sets out mandatory requirements that all advertisers — including healthcare professionals — must follow when promoting therapeutic goods to consumers.

The Code has two main parts:

**Part 1** applies to advertising of all therapeutic goods to consumers. It sets general standards around truthfulness, substantiation of claims, and the prohibition of certain content — including content that exploits patients' fears or vulnerabilities.

**Part 2** applies specifically to advertising of restricted therapeutic goods, including certain Schedule 2 and Schedule 3 (pharmacist only) medicines. It imposes additional requirements on what claims can be made and how.

It's worth noting that the Code is not a voluntary guideline. Breaching it can result in complaints to the TGA's Complaints Resolution Panel, enforceable undertakings, or referral to the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC). In serious cases, civil penalties apply.

## What medical practitioners cannot advertise to the public

This is where many practitioners are caught off guard. Australian law places strict limits on the advertising of prescription-only medicines (Schedule 4 and Schedule 8) and certain other therapeutic goods directly to consumers.

Under section 42DL of the *Therapeutic Goods Act 1989*, it is unlawful to advertise a prescription medicine to the general public. This restriction is absolute — there are no exemptions for practitioners advertising their own services, and intent is irrelevant.

In practice, this means:

- **You cannot name or reference specific prescription medications in public-facing marketing.** This includes posts promoting treatments for weight loss, erectile dysfunction, menopause, skin conditions, or mental health if they name or clearly imply a specific prescription product.
- **Before-and-after imagery tied to specific treatments may constitute advertising** of the underlying therapeutic good, particularly if the treatment involves a prescription medicine or registered device.
- **Testimonials that reference specific medicines or devices** are almost always non-compliant, regardless of whether the patient gave consent.
- **Promoting "off-label" uses of prescription medicines** to the public is prohibited, even if the use is clinically accepted.

This is a harder line than many practitioners realise. Saying "we offer Ozempic for weight management" on your website is not a grey area — it is a clear breach.

## The most common TGA advertising violations by practitioners

Based on the kinds of content the TGA and its Complaints Resolution Panel regularly review, the most frequent violations by medical practitioners fall into a few predictable categories:

**1. Naming prescription medicines in promotional content**
Websites, social posts, and email campaigns that name specific prescription medicines — particularly in the context of cosmetic, weight management, or lifestyle medicine — remain the most common breach category.

**2. Unsubstantiated claims about therapeutic outcomes**
Statements like "the most effective treatment available" or "proven to reverse ageing" require robust clinical evidence to support them. Practitioners often rely on manufacturer materials without verifying whether those claims meet the Code's substantiation requirements.

**3. Patient testimonials referencing specific treatments**
The Code places tight restrictions on the use of testimonials in therapeutic goods advertising. Testimonials that endorse specific medicines or devices — even those that appear genuinely patient-generated — are typically non-compliant.

**4. Pricing and promotional offers tied to therapeutic goods**
Running promotions like "book your Botox this month and receive a discount" intersects with TGA advertising rules, particularly where the product is a registered therapeutic good.

**5. Influencer and third-party posts**
If a practitioner sponsors, encourages, or provides content to a third party (including an influencer or patient ambassador), and that content promotes a therapeutic good, the practitioner may bear responsibility for compliance.

## Social media and TGA compliance — the grey areas

Social media is where TGA compliance becomes genuinely complex — and where many well-intentioned practitioners inadvertently breach the rules.

A few of the most contested areas:

**Educational content vs. advertising**
Not all content about therapeutic goods constitutes "advertising." Genuinely educational posts — explaining how a medicine works, discussing clinical evidence in a balanced way, or addressing patient questions without promotional intent — may not meet the legal definition of an advertisement. But the line between education and promotion is not always clear, and the TGA will look at the overall impression created, not just the practitioner's intent.

**Replying to patient comments**
If a patient comments on your post asking "do you offer X?" and you respond affirmatively in a public thread, that exchange may constitute advertising of a therapeutic good. Practitioners should consider redirecting these conversations to private consultations.

**Reposting patient content**
Sharing patient-generated posts, "user generated content," or tagging happy patients in results-based content can all carry compliance risk, particularly if therapeutic goods are explicitly or implicitly referenced.

**Platform-based targeting**
Using Meta or Google ad targeting to reach specific patient demographics with content about therapeutic goods raises additional issues. Even if the content itself is compliant, targeted delivery to certain audiences may heighten TGA scrutiny.

## How to stay TGA compliant in your marketing

Compliance doesn't mean abandoning digital marketing — it means building the right habits and checks into your content workflow.

**Know what you're advertising.** Before publishing any content that mentions a medicine, device, or treatment, ask whether that content could reasonably be seen as promoting a therapeutic good. If yes, the TGA advertising rules apply.

**Avoid naming prescription medicines in public content.** As a general rule, keep specific prescription product names out of public-facing marketing — including websites, social posts, and email. You can discuss conditions, treatment approaches, and consultation processes without crossing into prohibited advertising territory.

**Substantiate every claim.** Every therapeutic claim — whether about efficacy, safety, or outcomes — needs to be backed by credible, current evidence. Manufacturer data sheets are a starting point, but they are not always sufficient under the Code.

**Get a compliance check before you publish.** The volume of content practitioners publish has made manual compliance review impractical for most clinics. **Review your existing content.** Many practitioners have historic content on their websites and social channels that predates their awareness of TGA advertising rules. A periodic audit of existing content is worthwhile, particularly for clinics operating in high-risk practice areas like aesthetics, weight management, or sports medicine.

**Seek legal advice for complex situations.** Where your marketing involves significant investment — such as a product launch, a sponsored campaign, or a new clinical service — consider engaging a healthcare lawyer or your medical defence organisation before going live.

TGA advertising compliance is not optional, and the regulatory environment is becoming more active, not less. The good news is that with the right knowledge and a consistent pre-publication review process, the risk of inadvertent breaches is genuinely manageable — without sacrificing effective, patient-focused marketing.
