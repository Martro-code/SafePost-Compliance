Many Australian medical practitioners are aware that AHPRA regulates how they advertise their services. Fewer are aware that the penalties for getting it wrong are significantly higher than they used to be — or that the rules extend far beyond traditional advertising into social media, email signatures, and even how you respond to patient comments online.

If you advertise a regulated health service in Australia, this is what you need to know about the current penalty framework, how AHPRA enforces it, and what the consequences of a breach can look like in practice.

## How the penalty framework changed — and when

For many years, the maximum penalties for AHPRA advertising breaches were relatively modest: $5,000 per offence for an individual and $10,000 for a body corporate. Many practitioners treated compliance as a low-stakes administrative matter as a result.

That changed in 2022. The *Health Practitioner Regulation National Law and Other Legislation Amendment Act 2022* received assent on 21 October 2022, increasing maximum advertising penalties twelvefold — to $60,000 per offence for an individual and $120,000 per offence for a body corporate. The stated rationale was explicitly to deter non-compliant advertising through meaningful financial consequences.

These penalties applied in most states and territories from the time of the amendment. As of July 2024, they apply in all Australian jurisdictions, including Western Australia, which previously operated under different penalty thresholds under its own corresponding legislation.

The penalty increases are now fully in effect, nationally, and there is no transitional grace period.

## What the penalties actually apply to

Penalties apply to breaches of section 133 of the *Health Practitioner Regulation National Law*, which makes it an offence to advertise a regulated health service in a way that:

- Is false, misleading, or deceptive — or is likely to be
- Offers a gift, discount, or other inducement without clearly stating the terms and conditions
- Uses testimonials or purported testimonials about the clinical aspects of a service
- Creates an unreasonable expectation of beneficial treatment
- Directly or indirectly encourages the indiscriminate or unnecessary use of health services

Each of these is a separate offence — and each can attract its own penalty. A single social media post that contains a misleading claim, implies a guaranteed outcome, and references a patient testimonial could technically constitute multiple offences, each attracting up to $60,000 for an individual.

Penalties are not limited to registered practitioners. Anyone who advertises a regulated health service — including clinic owners, practice managers, or marketing staff acting on behalf of a practitioner — may be liable.

## Protected title misuse: the higher-stakes offence

Separate from advertising penalties, there is a distinct offence category for the misuse of protected professional titles — using "surgeon," "specialist," or other protected titles without holding the relevant registration.

The penalties here carry the same financial ceiling — up to $60,000 per offence for an individual and $120,000 for a body corporate — but also carry the possibility of imprisonment of up to three years per offence, or both a fine and imprisonment. This is a criminal offence, not a civil matter.

Practitioners operating in cosmetic medicine, where the use of titles like "cosmetic surgeon" has been a long-standing area of regulatory focus, should be particularly attentive to this.

## How AHPRA actually enforces advertising breaches

Understanding the penalty ceiling is one thing. Understanding how AHPRA actually exercises its enforcement powers is equally important.

AHPRA takes a risk-based approach. Not every breach results in prosecution — the regulator prioritises matters based on the potential for public harm, the seriousness of the conduct, and whether the practitioner has demonstrated willingness to comply.

In practice, the enforcement escalation typically looks like this:

**Warning letter.** For most first-identified breaches, AHPRA writes to the practitioner identifying the non-compliant content and providing a timeframe to correct it. This is the most common outcome for lower-risk matters.

**Show cause letter.** Where a practitioner fails to address non-compliance after being notified, AHPRA may issue a show cause letter proposing to impose conditions on registration — including conditions that restrict the practitioner's ability to advertise their services at all.

**Prosecution.** For serious, persistent, or high-risk breaches, AHPRA will prosecute. The penalties cited above are court-ordered outcomes. AHPRA has prosecuted advertising breaches in Australian courts, and the cases are a matter of public record.

**Disciplinary action.** For registered practitioners, advertising breaches can also trigger disciplinary proceedings before a tribunal — separate from, and in addition to, any criminal or civil penalty. This can result in conditions on registration, suspension, or cancellation.

The key phrase to understand is *per offence*. AHPRA does not assess a single penalty for a non-compliant campaign — it can assess a separate penalty for each piece of non-compliant content identified. Practitioners with a large body of non-compliant historic content across a website and multiple social channels face compounding exposure, not a single fixed fine.

## What counts as advertising — and why it's broader than you think

One reason practitioners are caught off guard by advertising enforcement is that the definition of "advertising" under the National Law is much broader than most people assume.

AHPRA's guidelines define advertising as covering all forms of verbal, printed, or electronic public communication that promotes a regulated health service provider to attract a person to the provider. This includes:

- Websites and landing pages
- Social media profiles and posts — including comments and replies
- Paid digital advertising
- Email marketing and newsletters
- Business cards, letterheads, and signage
- Media appearances and interviews in which services are promoted
- Email signatures

This means that a practitioner who replies to a patient comment on Instagram, sends a practice newsletter, or appears on a podcast talking about their services is engaging in conduct that falls within the advertising framework — and must comply with section 133 accordingly.

## The reputational dimension

Beyond the financial penalties, AHPRA publishes summaries of compliance and enforcement action. A practitioner who is prosecuted or has conditions imposed on their registration for advertising breaches is likely to have that matter publicly disclosed. For any practitioner whose reputation is integral to their referral base or patient acquisition, the reputational consequences of a public enforcement outcome may exceed the financial penalty itself.

This is particularly relevant in specialist medicine, cosmetic practice, and any area where patient trust is a primary differentiator.

## The practical takeaway

The penalty framework as it now stands treats advertising compliance as a genuine legal obligation — not an administrative formality. A single piece of non-compliant content can result in a fine that meaningfully affects a practitioner's finances. Multiple pieces of non-compliant content, published over time and identified in an AHPRA audit, can compound that exposure significantly.
